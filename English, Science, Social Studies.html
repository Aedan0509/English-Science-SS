The Science We Cannot Ignore

Yes, the Earth has natural cycles of warming and cooling. Volcanic eruptions can darken skies, and solar activity can shift temperatures. But the speed and intensity of today’s warming cannot be explained by nature alone. The current rapid rise in global temperatures is overwhelmingly linked to human activity, with multiple independent lines of evidence confirming that burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial emissions are the primary drivers. Global average temperatures have already risen about 1.14 C above pre-industrial levels, and the past decade has been the hottest on record. Temperature records also show NASA’s data (2023-2024) that the Earth's average surface temperature reached the highest levels since recordkeeping began in 1880.

Natural causes: Volcanic eruptions release ash and gases that can temporarily cool the Earth. Solar cycles also influence climate, but these changes are gradual and cannot explain the sharp warming trend of recent decades.

What we consider as a human activity that drives this: One example is burning fossil fuels that fills the air with carbon dioxide, trapping heat like a blanket around the Earth.Carbon dioxide traps heat by absorbing radiation and re-emitting it back toward the earth, preventing heat from escaping into space Another one is deforestation that removes the forests that act as lungs, absorbing carbon and releasing oxygen. Livestock and industrial production release methane and other gases, far more potent than CO₂ in heating the atmosphere. Methane is far more potent than CO₂ in trapping heat: over 20 years, methane warms the planet about 80+ times more than CO₂, though it stays in the atmosphere for a shorter time. This makes methane a critical target for immediate climate action, especially from livestock and industrial sources.

This is why the Philippines now faces hotter summers, stronger typhoons, and unpredictable rainfall. Science tells us plainly: the greenhouse effect is intensifying, and we are the ones fueling it.

How It Touches Our Lives

Climate change is not abstract, it is felt in the sweat of farmers by rising temperatures and unpredictable rainfall that reduces crop yields , the tears of families displaced by floods that destroys home, infrastructure, and livelihoods, forcing migration, and the empty nets of fisherfolk who lose their primary source of income, affecting local markets and trade.

🌾 Agriculture

Rice yields are projected to drop by up to 10% in the coming years At first, farmers may notice smaller harvests in certain seasons. Over time, repeated droughts and floods weaken soil fertility, forcing families to spend more on fertilizers or abandon farming altogether. . According to the Asian Development Bank’s Climate Risk Country Profile: Philippines, rising temperatures and erratic rainfall patterns are expected to reduce staple crop productivity, particularly rice, making food inflation a growing concern.

🐟 Fisheries

Warmer seas don’t push fish away overnight. At first, fish migrate slightly deeper or farther from shore, making daily catches smaller. Over time, traditional fishing grounds become empty, forcing fisherfolk to travel longer distances, spend more on fuel, and risk their safety at sea. This gradual shift erodes incomes, weakens coastal economies, and raises the price of seafood in local markets.

🏥 Health

Heatwaves may start as uncomfortable summers, but repeated exposure increases risks of heatstroke, respiratory illness, and dehydration. Floods initially spread waterborne diseases in affected barangays, but over time, repeated outbreaks strain hospitals and public health systems.The cumulative effect is a population more vulnerable to illness, with higher medical costs and lost productivity.

🏚 Infrastructure

Typhoons like Yolanda (Haiyan) destroyed billions worth of homes, schools, and roads, leaving scars that still remind us of our vulnerability.The cycle of destruction and rebuilding drains government budgets, leaving less money for development projects that could lift communities out of poverty.

One sector's failure causes a chain reaction in others. "This creates a negative Multiplier Effect: when fishermen lose income, they stop buying goods from the informal street vendors, who then cannot pay for transportation (services), causing a localized economic slowdown.

Inflation: The Silent Partner of Climate Change

When a typhoon wipes out crops, food prices rise. When floods damage power plants, electricity bills climb. Inflation is the silent partner of climate change, and it affects every sector: Category-5 typhoons in the Philippines raise food inflation by nearly 1% within a quarter after impacts. These shocks ripple across all sectors: Cost-push inflation happens when the cost of production rises, forcing businesses to increase prices. Unlike demand-pull inflation (where prices rise because consumers are buying more), cost-push inflation is driven by supply-side shocks.

Price Controls: Protecting Families in Crisis

During disasters, the government must step in to protect the most vulnerable. Price controls on essentials, rice, vegetables, fish, fuel, medicines, are not luxuries, they are lifelines. Without them, families already affected by floods or typhoons face exploitation in the marketplace. Infrastructure repair, subsidies, and price controls are vital, but they need to be complemented by monetary policy to stabilize the broader economy and protect households. BSP can lower reserve requirements, ensuring credit flows to businesses and households affected by disasters.

Disasters demand teamwork. Fiscal policy protects families. Monetary policy protects the economy.Together, they help us rebuild, not just structures, but lives.

What Government Must Do

Aspiring public servants must see climate action as economic action. Here are urgent steps:

Build food reserves and manage imports to stabilize supply.

Economic issue: Stabilizes food prices and prevents inflation during supply shocks.

Climate issue: Provides resilience against crop failures caused by droughts, floods, or typhoons, ensuring communities don’t face hunger when local harvests are disrupted.

Invest in renewable energy to reduce dependence on volatile fossil fuel markets.

Economic issue: Reduces exposure to unstable fossil fuel markets, lowering import costs and shielding economies from global price swings.

Climate issue: Cuts greenhouse gas emissions, slowing global warming and reducing air pollution.

Strengthen infrastructure so roads, bridges, and power systems can withstand stronger storms.

Economic issue: Protects trade routes, power systems, and productivity from costly damage, reducing repair expenses and economic losses.

Climate issue: Ensures roads, bridges, and grids can withstand stronger storms, floods, and heatwaves, minimizing disaster impacts.

Expand social protection, cash transfers, insurance, and livelihood programs for vulnerable communities.

Economic issue: Provides financial stability for vulnerable households, preventing poverty traps and maintaining consumer demand during crises.

Climate issue: Helps communities recover faster from climate-related disasters, reducing long-term vulnerability and dependence on emergency aid.

Encourage green industries through carbon pricing and incentives.

Economic issue: Stimulates innovation, creates jobs, and positions the economy for long-term competitiveness in sustainable markets.

Climate issue: Discourages high-emission practices while rewarding clean technologies, accelerating the transition to a low-carbon economy.

What We Can Do as Citizens

The government cannot act alone. Each of us has power:

  • Conserve energy and shift to renewables where possible. Renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro) produces little to no carbon emissions, helping slow global warming.
  • Plant trees and support reforestation. Reforestation restores ecosystems, improves biodiversity, and prevents soil erosion.
  • Reduce waste, recycle, and choose sustainable products. Recycling conserves raw materials and energy that would otherwise be used in production.
  • Speak up, advocate for climate-conscious policies in barangays and municipalities. Community voices can push leaders to prioritize sustainability and resilience in planning.

Small acts, multiplied across millions of households, become a force for change. In short, households are the smallest unit of society, but when choices align, they become a collective force that reshapes markets, policies, and cultural norms.

A Call to Aspiring Public Servants

Climate change is not a distant storm, it is already here, reshaping the economy and daily lives of Filipinos. Every typhoon that destroys crops, every flood that displaces families, every heatwave that raises electricity bills is a reminder that climate action is economic survival.

As future leaders, you hold the responsibility to act boldly. To invest in resilience. To protect the vulnerable. To champion sustainable growth. Every peso spent on climate adaptation today saves lives, livelihoods, and billions in economic losses tomorrow.

The fight against climate change is also a fight for justice, dignity, and survival. The question is not whether we can afford to act, the truth is, we cannot afford not to.
