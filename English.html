Climate change is not just about rising temperatures or melting ice caps in faraway places. It is about the price of rice in the market, the cost of electricity in our homes, and the stability of jobs in our communities. For aspiring public servants, this is the challenge of our time: to see climate change not only as an environmental crisis but as an economic one that touches every Filipino family.

The Science We Cannot Ignore

Yes, the Earth has natural cycles of warming and cooling. Volcanic eruptions can darken skies, and solar activity can shift temperatures. But the speed and intensity of today’s warming cannot be explained by nature alone. The current rapid rise in global temperatures is overwhelmingly linked to human activity, with multiple independent lines of evidence confirming that burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial emissions are the primary drivers. Global average temperatures have already risen about 1.14 C above pre-industrial levels, and the past decade has been the hottest on record. Temperature records also show NASA’s data (2023-2024) that the Earth's average surface temperature reached the highest levels since recordkeeping began in 1880.

Natural causes: Volcanic eruptions release ash and gases that can temporarily cool the Earth. Solar cycles also influence climate, but these changes are gradual and cannot explain the sharp warming trend of recent decades.

What we consider as a human activity that drives this: One example is burning fossil fuels that fills the air with carbon dioxide, trapping heat like a blanket around the Earth.Carbon dioxide traps heat by absorbing radiation and re-emitting it back toward the earth, preventing heat from escaping into space Another one is deforestation that removes the forests that act as lungs, absorbing carbon and releasing oxygen. Livestock and industrial production release methane and other gases, far more potent than CO₂ in heating the atmosphere. Methane is far more potent than CO₂ in trapping heat: over 20 years, methane warms the planet about 80+ times more than CO₂, though it stays in the atmosphere for a shorter time. This makes methane a critical target for immediate climate action, especially from livestock and industrial sources.

This is why the Philippines now faces hotter summers, stronger typhoons, and unpredictable rainfall. Science tells us plainly: the greenhouse effect is intensifying, and we are the ones fueling it.

How It Touches Our Lives

Climate change is not abstract, it is felt in the sweat of farmers by rising temperatures and unpredictable rainfall that reduces crop yields , the tears of families displaced by floods that destroys home, infrastructure, and livelihoods, forcing migration, and the empty nets of fisherfolk who lose their primary source of income, affecting local markets and trade.

What Government Must Do

Build Food Reserves

Stabilizes food prices, prevents hunger.

Invest in Renewable Energy

Cuts emissions, shields economy from fossil fuel volatility.

Strengthen Infrastructure

Protects trade routes and power systems.

Expand Social Protection

Cash transfers and insurance for vulnerable families.

Encourage Green Industries

Creates jobs, accelerates low-carbon transition.

What We Can Do as Citizens

  • Conserve energy and shift to renewables
  • Plant trees and support reforestation
  • Reduce waste, recycle, and choose sustainable products
  • Advocate for climate-conscious policies in barangays
